# Executive Summary
In my experience, the simplest way to get better at running is built on three key workouts, in order of importance:
1. A long run
2. Intervals
3. Hills

Thats it. Do those three runs each week consistently, and you will improve. Keep it up for a year, and you will improve _a lot_. This basic approach can work for any distance from 3km to marathon.

Consistency is key. Getting running fit is like blowing up a leaky balloon. Regular, consistent effort works best. Too fast and the balloon will pop (ie, you will get injured). Too slow and so much air will have leaked out that you'll be going backwards. Every session serves to make you a little bit fitter, and over time your fitness will naturally erode. 

Once you have caught the running bug (and I guess the bug is why you are here), the biggest problem with consistency is injury. Injury is the bane of the runner, and is something I have struggled with my entire life. So the real key to progress is to be able to train consistently, in a way that avoids injury. 

Being impatient is the most common cause of injury. By that, I mean trying to get fast too quickly, by increasing volume or intensity faster than your body can adapt. Running is (ironically) a sport that demands patience. You need to think in seasons, halves, or years. Thinking in weeks or months is too short term and is asking for trouble. 

Training works by allowing the body to recover from stress. The training cycle is stress > recover > adapt. This is how your body gets stronger, more efficient, faster, and develops more endurance. 

Prevention is better than cure. The most effective injury prevention is to find your minimum effective dose - ie, what is the minimum amount of load needed in this session to get your body to adapt. There is probably a range of adaption to loads - ie, if the load is only just enough to trigger an adaptation, you will get a small improvement. Increasing the load will increase the response of your body, but there is also a limit to the adaptation your body can make, and any load beyond this is detrimental, serving only to increase your risk of injury.
