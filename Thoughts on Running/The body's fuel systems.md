# The body's fuel systems

You are running comfortably below your aerobic threshold, your muscles are ticking along nicely, your oxygen supply is keeping up - why can't you just keep going indefinitely? What eventually stops you?

Fuel is probably the answer.

Your body likes to run off carbohydrate. When you eat, your body breaks the food down and sends it around the body, where is gets stored in muscles and in the liver as glycogen. When you exercise, you use this stored supply. The more you exercise, the more your body stores - one of the many adaptations that training creates.

But even the best trained athletes have a physical limit. The body can only store so much energy - in my experience (and I am far from alone) just enough to get you to the 30km point of a marathon. Running out of glycogen is what we fondly term "hitting the wall", "bonking", "carrying the piano", or "piggybacking the elephant". And those terms are not even exaggerations - your body literally runs out of petrol and your power output drops by what feels like 90%.

There are two strategies available
* Prevention
* Mitigation

## Prevention
Just, don't run out of fuel? Sure. So point one, the better trained you are, the more fuel your body will have (and the more efficiently it will use it). But if even the best runners will run out, what else can we do?
Eat.
Ingest carbohydrates, and have your body refuel while you are running. This is why runners eat gu's, have carbohydrates drinks, drink coke, eat boiled potatoes etc etc. The goal is to just get as much carbs into your body while you are running intensely as you can. But be sure not to upset your stomach. Shoving things in your mouth while you are running is not the hard part, having the gut that can digest it is the hard part. This too requires training. And as we will cover in race prep - nothing new on race day. This means either taking your own fuel to eat, or practising with what will be provided on race day beforehand. Rob de Castella had an unfortunate race day incident when he tried eating something new during a race that didn't agree with him. It was not pretty.

Note that the point of prevention is not to wait until you hit the wall to start eating - you will want to start early (even before the race starts) and consume relatively evenly throughout the event. (Although if you do have the misfortune of meeting a wall, eating will help get things back on track).

[This](https://marathonhandbook.com/how-sabastian-sawe-fueled-his-sub-two-hour-marathon-london/) is a super interesting read about how Sebastian Sawe fuelled his sub two hour marathon, to give an idea of how serious this aspect is in elite events.

## Mitigation
When you run out of carbohydrates, you slow down, but the lights stay on. You can still walk and talk and breathe. This is probably because your body doesn't let you use all the fuel you have, but also because you have an alternate fuel source - fat.
While the body will preference carbs, if there are none available it will use fat to power exercise. With training, fat can be almost as effective an energy sources as carbs are - but most runners almost never train using fat as a fuel. This is because when carbs are in your body, that is what will be used. In order to target fat as an energy source, you need to deplete yourself entirely of carbs - either by not eating them in the first place (think the keto diet, atkins diet, lchf, etc), or by doing your long run fasted. ie, avoid carbs at dinner the night before and then do your long run in the morning, avoiding all carbs. If your body's store is low enough, and you run far enough, you will force it to start using fat. And like any training, the more you do it, the more your body will adapt to make it efficient.
With enough practice, your body will become proficient at switching between the two energy sources, greatly reducing the consequences of running out of carbs.

## When to think about fuelling?
If you have done reasonable training, any race under an hour should be fine without the need to fuel. 

For races between 1 and 1.5 hours you might want to consider fuelling, but it is not critical.

For races over 1.5 hours, I recommend definitely thinking about what and when to eat. Practice a few different types of food and / or drinks during your long run and see what you like. Then think about how often you want to consume them, and whether to measure that by time or by distance. Make sure what you train with is what you race with - ie, if the race provides a certain brand or mix of energy drink / gel, either train with that or carry your own on race day!

Note that the gel companies (surprisingly!) will recommend that you buy and eat a lot of gels. Don't feel obligated to follow their recommendations. Like anything, start small and gradually increase until you reach the minimum effective dose. 

(Side note - keep an eye on the caffeine content of some gels! If you eat a few without thinking, you might be taking on the equivalent of 8 or 9 coffees!)
