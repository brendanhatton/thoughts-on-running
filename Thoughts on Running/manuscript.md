# Executive Summary
The simplest way to get better at running is built on three key workouts, in order of importance:
1. A long run
2. Intervals
3. Hills

Thats it. Do those three runs each week consistently, and you will improve. Keep it up for a year, gradually increasing the load, and you will improve _a lot_. This basic approach can work for any distance from 3km to marathon.

Consistency is key. Getting running fit is like blowing up a leaky balloon. Regular, consistent effort works best. Too fast, and the balloon will pop (ie, you will get injured). Too slow, and so much air will have leaked out that you'll be going backwards. Every session serves to make you a little bit fitter, and over time your fitness will naturally return to its baseline. 

Once you have caught the running bug (and I guess the bug is why you are here), the biggest problem with consistency is injury. Injury is the bane of the runner, and is something I have struggled with my entire life. So the real key to progress is to be able to train consistently, in a way that avoids injury. 

Being impatient is the most common cause of injury. By that, I mean trying to get fast too quickly, by increasing volume or intensity faster than your body can adapt. Running is (ironically) a sport that demands patience. You need to think in seasons, halves, or years. Thinking in weeks or months is too short term and is asking for trouble. 

This long term outlook means that to become a sustainable activity - one you enjoy, and persist with for years or decades, means that you need to enjoy the journey, not just the destination. You need to learn to enjoy the activity of running, not just being focused on the next race, or beating your best time. Run off road, run through the forest, or up a mountain, run at dawn, run with friends, run to explore a new place, enter running events of all sorts. Try running related activities like orienteering or adventure racing. Don't stress about the numbers your watch tells you, listen to your body and enjoy it for what it is. Once you discover the joy of running, you'll never want to stop.

Training works by allowing the body to recover from stress. The training cycle is stress > recover > adapt. This is how your body gets stronger, more efficient, faster, and develops more endurance. 

Prevention is better than cure, in most of life, but specifically referring to injuries. The most effective injury prevention is to find your minimum effective dose - ie, what is the minimum amount of load needed in this session to get your body to adapt. There is probably a range of adaption to loads - ie, if the load is only just enough to trigger an adaptation, you will get a small improvement. Increasing the load will increase the response of your body, but there is also a limit to the adaptation your body can make, and any load beyond this is detrimental, serving only to increase your risk of injury. So finding what training load gives you good adaptation with minimal injury risk is the goal of effective training.

# Who is this guide for?
This guide is for most casual / amateur runners. If you are just starting out and can't yet run more than a few hundred metres, I suggest following the [Couch to 5km plan](https://c25k.com/).

This guide is intentionally quite basic, so will be of limited use to the other extreme end of the running spectrum - ie, elite runners, or those targeting specialised ultra running events. 

I'd like to think there is something to offer for everyone else that wants to continue progressing their running journey!

# Getting started
I have been running for as long as I can remember. I didn't remember the feeling of starting from scratch, until my first big injury. I couldn't run at all for months. When I finally got the all clear, it was like I had never run, and I was starting from the couch. And it _sucked_. It suddenly dawned on me that this is what other people felt when they started running! This is why everyone else seems to complain about running, and thinks I am crazy for loving it. They don't know that _it gets better_. So, so much better! But starting out is hard. It hurts, it's uncomfortable, it's not fun, why would anyone keep it up?
Well trust me, it's worth it. Just push through a few months of discomfort, and a whole world will open up to you, for the rest of your life. Once you are fit, running feels fantastic. There is a reason why running addicts are literally addicted - because it is awesome.

Some tips to help you get going:
1. Use a structured program like c25k.com/c25k_plan/. Note that this plan is fairly intense, so don't stress if you need to space your sessions out further. You might take longer to get there, but keep it up and you absolutely will. Consistency is key.
2. Do the journey with someone else. Having a training partner, or joining a running group (some have specific courses for people starting out), or convincing someone to join you when they can helps with the motivation a lot.
3. Find a dedicated, regular time. Everyone is busy, nobody has lots of spare time. But find a time that works for you and commit to it. Once you miss a few sessions, it is so easy to become demotivated and give up. But you can't reach that point if you don't miss the first one! Really commit, especially for the first few months when the habit is still forming.
4. Bribe yourself. Get a coffee and / or croissant afterwards. Have a chocolate. Whatever short term bribe you need to get through the start and to the point where you can enjoy running for what it is. The long term exercise benefit once you form the habit will far outweigh any short term sugar based bribery you need to get there.
5. Once you can cover 5kms, look into a local [parkrun](https://www.parkrun.com/). A free (completely free), casual 5km run (or walk) every week - and there are thousands around the globe. It's a great way to start the weekend, and can make a great commitment to a regular running routine.

# Patience
It is ironic that running is not for the impatient. There are no shortcuts. The only way to improve at running is to do the work. Now there are some ways to do work that are more efficient and effective than others, and that is what I hope to share, but ultimately most people need to reset their expectations. Thinking you will see measurable improvements every week is unrealistic and will set you up for disappointment.
At most you might think of a monthly check in, but better is to think about where you want to be in 6 months or this time next year. 

Consistency is your friend. You want to be sustainable, consistent and enjoy the journey, not just focus on the destination. 

Trying to run too far, too fast, will guarantee illness and / or injury, forcing you to stop running and slowing down your long term progress. 

To give some perspective, a famous Australian running coach once said "after a runner has been on a high volume program (150kms+ per week) for three or four years, then you'll know what they are capable of". 

---

# Consistency
Consistency is the single biggest factor influencing your progress as a runner. Being able to run every week, with a gradual increase in load over time, is the best, safest, and most sustainable way to progress. Gradually, sustained improvement will outperform short term spikes of inconsistent progress.

[Robert de Castella](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_de_Castella) once had a 1000 day training streak. When Jeff Hunt made the fastest Australian men's marathon debut on record, he had not missed a single long run for years (even turning up the day after his bucks party). [Eliud Kipchoge](https://run.outsideonline.com/training/training-plans/marathon/eliud-kipchoge-marathon-workout-training-principles/) performs basically the same weekly training pattern week in, week out.

Once you are addicted to running (when, not if), the biggest impediment to consistency will be injury. Some research quotes an injury rate of [65-80%](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Road_running#Impact) of runners per year, so being able to run regularly while avoiding injury is the holy grail.

Running fitness is like inflating a leaky balloon. Each workout is blowing a bit of air into the balloon. But there is a small hole, and over time air will also leak out. Inconsistent training will allow more and more air to leak out, and trying to blow too much air in too fast will lead to the balloon tearing or popping - ie, you getting injury. Steady and consistent progress is the best way.

# Three dimensions
Running fitness has three broad dimensions:
1. Endurance
2. Speed
3. Strength

## Endurance
Endurance means how far you can cover, or how long you can keep running for. Hitting the wall or running out of gas is a common feeling for reaching the limit of your endurance, your body simply cannot keep going.

## Speed
How fast can you run? What speed can you sustain for a medium distance? What speed can you maintain for a long distance? Training for speed is different to training for endurance, it is a different dimension.

## Strength
Being a strong runner refers to how well you can handle hills, headwinds, running on sand or off road. Anything that means your muscles need to work harder than they would in ideal conditions. 
A runner that isn't strong will dread a hill, because their heart rate will skyrocket, their legs turn to jelly, and they will have to slow down. 
A strong runner will look forward to hills because everyone else will slow down and they will move ahead in a race.

## Limiting factor
You can only run a race as fast as your *slowest* dimension. ie, you might have endurance to run all day, but if your maximum sustainable speed is slow, you will only be able to finish the race slowly. If you have speed galore, but can only cover 2kms before hitting the wall, that 10km run is _really_ going to hurt. That is why it is important to train in all three dimensions, and that is what the three key workouts do, and why this approach is so effective.

# Long run
The long run is the most important session of the week, meaning that it offers the most benefit and produces the greatest improvement. It mostly targets the endurance dimensions of fitness, but will benefit the other dimensions too.

A long run is just that, a run that is significantly longer than your other runs. What I mean by long is 90 - 150 minutes, or 16 - 25kms. Running this distance forces your body to adapt in several ways. You will increase in endurance, your body will store more fuel, your muscles will be able to function for longer, and your aerobic system (heart, lungs and oxygen based muscle activity) will become more efficient. This is the most valuable run because it creates so many adaptations in the body.

## How to start
Of course having read the importance of patience and consistency, you wouldn't jump in to anything significantly longer than your current sessions, would you? Good, I thought not.

Start by picking a day that gives you enough time. Sunday morning is the most popular time for long runs, but whenever works for you. Make your first run the same distance as you have been running. The next week, increase it by five minutes. Then next week, a few minutes more. Continue gradually and consistently.

If you have to miss a week, don't stress. If you can swap one of your other sessions for the long run, do that. If you can't, just replay the session you would have done - don't try to catch up to the distance where you would have been without missing one. There are no shortcuts. 

Some [recent research](https://marathonhandbook.com/the-10-rule-new-study-suggests-weve-been-doing-it-wrong-this-whole-time/) links increased injury risk to how much any single run sticks out compared to the other runs over the past few weeks, meaning any spikes in the distance of your long runs is especially risky. So be sure to always increase the distances slowly and steadily.

[Jeff Galloway](https://www.jeffgalloway.com/), US Olympian, has pioneered an approach of mixing running and walking, now known as [Jeffing](https://www.jeffgalloway.com/training/run-walk/),  as a way to reduce the injury risk of long runs, without compromising the benefits. 

For races of half marathon distance or greater, the long run is also your chance to practice things like fuelling, pacing, shoes, and clothing. We will cover it later, but a common refrain that I strongly believe in is "nothing new on race day".

# Intervals
Intervals are short periods of high intensity running, mixed with periods of lower intensity, recovery jogs. The idea is that by running faster than you can currently sustain, your body will experience the load and demands of that increased intensity, and adapt to better handle it in the future - which means you will be able to run faster! This targets the speed dimension of running fitness.

Intervals are probably the most diverse session, with many different plans or suggestions, but the aim of all of them is to run fast for some relatively short distance, allow your body to recover somewhat, and then run fast again.

Some famous sessions include: 
* Rob de Castellas "Deek's Quarters".  8 x 400m with 200m jog recovery between each. Deek used to run them in 61-62s - good luck with that!
* Steve Monaghetti's "Mona fartlek"
	* 2 x 90 seconds hard, 90 seconds float 
	* 4 x 60 seconds hard, 60 seconds float
	* 4 x 30 seconds hard, 30 seconds float
	* 4 x 15 seconds hard, 15 seconds float

But you can really make this whatever you like. The ideal pace to aim for is that your times are even, and the last one you do is the last one you _could_ do (once you have built up to that level of exertion - the first few sessions should leave you with plenty in the tank). Make sure you hold your form - this is training and not a life or death race. If you push so hard that your form suffers, you are trying too hard and need to slow down and / or allow more recovery time.

As you increase the load here, be careful to increase either intensity or distance, or decrease the recovery - but only one at a time. You either add cover more distance, or you push your body harder, not both.

Most runners have only a small part of their training as high intensity, so intervals should only form a small part of your overall weekly kilometres. This is because high intensity work is considered a high risk of injury - but they pack a punch! Think of is as the chilli powder of running, to be used sparingly. That metaphor also works because it hurts, sort of in a good way. 

Some interesting research from [Lore of Running](https://www.runnersworld.com/advanced/a20805321/the-lore-of-running/) is that your body takes around two weeks to show any benefit from speed work. Which both gives an idea of how long improvements take to become visible or significant, but also a point that you should reduce speed work in the two weeks before any big race. More on this later in race preparation.

## How to get started
Even if you haven't run fast since playing tip in the primary school playground, it's not a problem - follow what is hopefully familiar advice by now: start slowly and gradually build up.
* Pick one session a week to be your interval session. 
* Do a warm up jog of 10-15 minutes.
* Depending on how intensely you plan to run and / or how nervous you are, do a few run throughs - this is a short (~50 - 100m) effort which starts as a jog and builds up to faster pace. You might do three run throughs increasing your effort each time. The first only reach about 75% of your maximum speed, the second 85% and the third 90-95%. 
* Your body will be ready for your interval session.
* Start small! You can just start with 2 or 3 reps of your chosen distance, and the speed can simply be a fast jog. 
* After each effort, either walk back, or walk for a minute or two until your breathing returns to normal, then do your next rep. 
* The next week you can go a bit harder and / or add a rep depending on how you felt after your last session. 
* Gradually build up, alternating between increasing the intensity (either by running faster, or reducing the recover), or adding distance by more reps / making your reps longer.

# Hills
Hills are the third of the key workouts, and deliberately so. While hill sessions generally contribute less than either long runs or intervals, they are still very valuable. 

Having the strength to take on hills without having your body freak out feels really good. Hills are also a good way to strengthen your muscles and tendons without needing to run too far.

Most races have some hills, and some seem to deliberately seek out as much as they can ([six foot](https://www.sixfoot.com/caves-house-finish-elevation-and-map), [coast to kosci](https://coasttokosci.com/course/), [uta](https://uta.utmb.world/races/UTA100), [utmb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultra-Trail_du_Mont-Blanc#Route) ) - so if you have a specific race in mind, definitely check out the profile and determine how much hill training you should incorporate.

Hills can be combined with either long runs or speed sessions (somewhat). A long, hilly run can work both dimensions, or short hill reps can (somewhat) help with both speed and strength.

For most casual runners, incorporating hills somewhere in your regular weekly runs will be enough. Just don't dodge them forever, because one day you'll get caught out!

## How to get started
So you've decided you want to be a strong runner. What not?
As always, start small and build gradually but consistently. 
### Option 1 - Hills as a session
If you want to add a hill specific session, the approach is similar to an interval session, but on a hill.
* Find a hill which is something between 50 and 100 metres long and has a 'decent' gradient. 
* The gradient should be enough that you know it's a hill, but doesn't have to be super steep. Ideally a consistent incline for the duration. 
* Given the natural variation of hills, you don't need to find a perfect one. Just find one that is convenient and is in the ballpark of the description above.
* Start small and slow - maybe 2 or 3 reps, depending on the steepness, length and speed you want to run.
* Do a warm up jog of 10-15 minutes.
* Run up the hill
	* Start with a jog (depending on the steepness of the slope, maybe a fast jog)
	* Focus on holding your form and being strong
	* Keep your head and torso up, shoulders open so your lungs have lots of air
	* Use your glutes and calves to power you forward
* The ideal pace is a similar goal to intervals, you want all your reps to be roughly equally paced, and you want to feel pretty tired by the end
* If you can't hold your form, or you are massively out of breath, you need to slow down. This is a strength session, if you can't keep the right muscles engaged, you won't get the benefit
* Once you finish the rep, either walk or jog back, and repeat
* The next week you can go a bit harder and / or add a rep depending on how you felt after your last session. 

### Option 2 - Hills as part of other sessions
* Not a lot of runners will do a specific hill session, but they will ensure to have plenty of hills in their other sessions
* On your long run, start planning it to include some hills
* When you are on the hills, remember to focus on your form and being strong
* Make sure you keep your head and torso up, and are using your glutes and calves
* The more hills you cover throughout your sessions, the stronger you will become
* If you only have one or two hills available, nothing stops you making that a loop that you repeat a few times in the middle of your run
* Prioritise the hill effort. You will gain more from pushing up the hill and then slowing down to recover than from slowing down as you climb the hill

# Injury prevention
I know pretty much the everything in this book talks about preventing injuries, but I wanted to collect the key ideas in one place. Well over half of people that run regularly will be injured each year - which is a huge number! So for you, it's a matter of when, not if. But some basic precautions can reduce your risk.

The cause of most running related injuries is "overuse", such a muscle tear, or a tendon that is very, very unhappy with you - but at a higher level, the problem is that different parts of your body adapt at different rates, and you can only safely increase the load at the rate of the slowest part. The aerobic system, and muscle strength can adapt relatively quickly, but other parts like tendons and bones only adapt much more slowly. So you will feel like you can run faster - and you can. For a little while. Most running related injuries are delayed 4-6 weeks after the load is increased. Your body can and will tolerate a rapid increase in load. For a little while. But it will catch up with you eventually. 

A related cause of problems is muscular imbalance - like when your hamstrings suddenly become 50% stronger (and tigher) than your quads. What used to be relatively balanced is now very unbalanced - and your knees will let you know about this! This is also contributed to by increasing training load faster than your body can safely adapt, as well as needing to do some strength and conditioning to keep your body in balance.

The major factors contributing to running injuries are running volume and running intensity.

## Volume
Running volume refers to how many kilometres you run each week. Whether that is 20km or 200km. The volume itself is not the biggest factor, but the rate at which the volume changes. ie, when you enter a race in three months time and suddenly ramp up your training, you are asking for trouble.

One rough rule of thumb is to not increase by more than 10% per week. This is a very rough guide, but gives an idea of how slowly you need to go about building your fitness. 

I would also add to this rule that you can only increase one of volume, intensity, or frequency at a time. ie, you can't start to run further and faster in the same time. And you can't add a new session if you are also increasing volume or intensity of your other workouts.

To give some sense of what different levels of volume actually look like: a casual runner might cover 20–30 kilometres a week. An enthusiastic amateur is probably somewhere in the 50–90 kilometre range. More than that, and you're in serious runner territory. Elite runners typically cover 150–200 kilometres a week, week after week, for years. Sabastian Sawe (who broke 2 hours for the marathon and world record holder) reportedly [exceeded 240 kilometres per week](https://theconversation.com/how-2-men-smashed-through-a-marathon-barrier-long-thought-unbreakable-281522). Which is, frankly, a number that is difficult to fully comprehend.

An interesting side effect about training that you might not appreciate at first - training does make you faster, but probably more beneficial is that makes your body capable of handling more training. And more training, done consistently over time, makes you faster still - in a compounding effect. This is one of the reasons the best runners in the world have often been running seriously for a decade or more — they've spent years building a base that allows them to train at volumes that would destroy a less-prepared body. [[Patience]] is key.

## Length of your longest long run
As described in [[Long run]], recent research shows that an even better predictor of injury than overall volume, is the difference between your longest run and your average run. ie, is there a spike or outlier in your longest run? In addition to being gradual and intentional about the growth of your overall volume, you need to be equally careful to control the growth of each training session.

## Intensity
Most runners do the majority of their runs at a relatively easy pace. Kilian Jornet (one of / the greatest ultra runner ever) [does 80% of his training in zone 1](https://marathonhandbook.com/kilian-jornet-is-back-at-western-states-hes-been-cooking-himself-to-get-ready/)! (Zone 1 is the lowest heart rate zone, zone 5 is the highest - so this is super low intensity). When its time to run hard, run hard, but most of the time run easy. Remember that you are seeking the minimum effective dose - you want to do the minimum amount of work needed to get your body to adapt, and most aerobic adaptations come from low intensity running. Consistency is where the benefit comes from, rather than effort.

For some people, that's easier to say than to do. I like to run fast. I like the feel like I am near my limit. And I believe this is primarily why I get injured a lot. If I could control myself better and learn to enjoy more easy jogging, I think I would be much better off and probably running a lot faster. This is another good reason to find a running group, or someone to run with to keep you in check.

## Conditioning
Once your running becomes a non-trivial amount, it is worth introducing some conditioning activities - ie, stretching and strengthening. Some people find yoga or pilates works for them. There are in person classes or heaps of videos (look for running specific ones). 
You can also find many resources with exercises to incorporate. Or if you are like me, you can rifle through your encyclopedia of physio-prescribed rehab exercises from your many, many visits.

The general idea is that you want some exercises targeting the major muscles:
* core
* hips
* glutes
* quads
* hamstrings
* calves

Like all training, introduce it gradually, make it consistent, and slowly increase the load.

# Recovery
Running doesn't make you faster or fitter. Recovery from running makes you faster and fitter.

The training itself is just the stimulus - the stress applied to your body that forces it to adapt. The changes (and improvements) actually happens during the recovery period that follows. When you rest, your muscles repair, your tendons strengthen, your cardiovascular system improves. Skip the recovery, and you're just repeatedly applying stress to a body that never quite gets the chance to respond to it. You'll feel tired, your performance will plateau or decline, and eventually something will break.

For most of us, this isn't really an issue, or is an easy on to solve - only run every second day. 

Only when you start to get a bit more serious, and need to start connecting days, does it need to be any more complex than that. At that point, the rule changes to: don't run hard two days in a row. And try to space the same type of run out as far as possible - ie separate your long runs, and separate your intervals as much as you can, to give those specific parts of your body the best chance to recover.

# How training works
Why does running make you faster?
When some physical load is put on our us, our bodies naturally adapt to be able to better handle that load in the future.

So lifting weights causes your body to grow more muscle fibres, which makes you better able to lift that weight (and larger weights) in the future.

Part of running is creating new muscle fibres, but the strongest people are not the faster runners, and vice versa.

In part, this is because of the [[Three dimensions]] of running fitness, ie strength is just one component. But there is a lot more detail about how training works.

Most runners operate in the aerobic zone, meaning the key factors are legs, lungs, and heart. But what actually changes when you train these?

## Heart
Your heart pumps the blood around your body. When you are exercising, your muscles need blood primarily to supply oxygen. A heart that is stronger and can pump more blood will make more oxygen available to your muscles. 
An indicator of your hearts condition is your resting heart rate. As you become fitter, your resting heart rate becomes lower - ie, you need fewer heartbeats to meet your body's requirements at rest.
Your maximum heart rate is a little different, it is unlikely to move much as a result of fitness. The rule of thumb for maximum heart rate is 220 minus your age. 

## Lungs
The heart pumping blood only helps exercise if that blood is carrying a full load of fresh oxygen. Lungs are where the body oxygenates the blood, allowing wastes like carbon dioxide out, and oxygen to get in. Exercise will improve the efficiency of your lungs, meaning they can add more oxygen to your blood, and you won't need to breathe as hard to support your exercise. 

# Legs
You legs are where the oxygen is put to work. As muscles contract, the consume oxygen in a reaction to power the contraction. A muscle is a collection of muscle fibres. Exercise can both stimulate more fibres to grow, and individual fibres to get stronger. In addition, muscles can become more efficient, requiring fewer inputs (eg oxygen) to produce the same output (the contraction).

(Side note - did you know muscles can only ever contract - ie pull. A muscle can never push, thats why almost all muscles are in pairs.)

## Endurance
Endurance is how long you can run at a reasonable pace for. Generally endurance comes into the picture when the intensity is low enough that you can run for over 30 minutes. Endurance is a factor of how long your muscles can operate for, and how long your body can fuel the run. 
As your muscles become more efficient and you have more muscle fibres, you can run for longer, as long as your body can fuel it. The primary fuel source is your liver, so as you train more, you liver learns to store more fuel, allowing you to exercise for longer.

## VO2 max
VO2 max is considered the best measure of how fit your aerobic system is. It is the maximal rate of oxygen consumption during exercise - ie, how much oxygen can your body get to its muscles, and how much can the muscles use. ie, tests the maximum throughput of legs, lungs and heart. 
Some point out that this does not directly correlate to fitness or running speed, because some muscles use oxygen more efficiently than others - so this is a relative indicator of aerobic fitness rather than something that can be directly compared between people. 

## Digestion
For most people when running, the blood supply is diverted away from the digestive system, which is why you might have a hard time forcing yourself to eat during intense exercise. However by practicing eating during training, your digestive system will also become more adapted and able to process the food you consume, making it available to sustain your workload.

At the time of writing, we are seeing endurance records fall across all sports . One of the big reasons for this is the science behind carbohydrate dense fuels - athletes can now eat and digest much higher amounts of carbohydrates than ever before. Plus training the body to handle this ingestion is recognised as just as important as training the rest of the body.

## Minimum effective dose
The purpose of each training session is to force some adaptation from the body - to get stronger and more efficient. The smallest level of training that yields that response is termed the [minimum effective dose](https://enduranceexplained.com/minimum-effective-dose-vs-maximum-effective-dose/). After you have trained enough to reach this level, any additional training will have very limited benefit, but greatly increased risk of injury. 

On the other hand, sometime that has reached a certain level of fitness and then repeats the exact same training load but expecting significant progress will likely be disappointed. As you become fitter and better adapted to running, the minimum effective dose needed to force additional adaptation grows. Growing fitter, faster, and stronger is expensive for your body, it takes energy, and requires rest. If your body can handle the training load in its current state, why would it spend the energy the adapt? You haven't stressed it enough to force it to adapt.

# The body's energy systems
I am not a scientist, and this is not a textbook, but having a crude, high level understanding of how the body works will help you understand how and why training works, and how you can optimise it for yourself.

The body has three systems it can use during running:
1. ATP-CP - Super intense, short duration. ie, sprinting. Most people have less than ten seconds of this available at a time.
2. Anaerobic - literally "without oxygen", this is the system for intense running that is not quite a sprint, but unsustainably intense, like a 400m. It is "without oxygen" because the body cannot supply enough oxygen to support the intensity of work demanded, so the muscles do their job without it. This comes with a friendly little side kick called lactic acid - aka jelly legs. 
3. Aerobic - the "with oxygen" variant, meaning the intensity is low enough that there is sufficient oxygen to power the work. This is what is in use for any races of more than a few minutes long.

Switching between this systems happens automatically, based on the intensity of the output being demanded of the body. Most runners are in the aerobic system almost all the time, but the barrier between aerobic and anaerobic is a blurry area known as the aerobic threshold, or the red zone. This is when you are at the upper limit of what your body can support by aerobic means, and you might not be able to sustain it for long, or it might burn you energy reserves faster than usual, leading to consequences later in the run.

One of the key objectives of interval training and long run training is not just to get physically faster, but to increase the intensity point at which the body will switch from aerobic to anaerobic. eg, if someone increases their aerobic threshold from 75% intensity to 80% intensity, they can now run 6% faster and still be in the aerobic system.

# The body's fuel systems
You are running comfortably below your aerobic threshold, your muscles are ticking along nicely, your oxygen supply is keeping up - why can't you just keep going indefinitely? What eventually stops you?

Fuel is probably the answer.

Your body likes to run off carbohydrate. When you eat, your body breaks the food down and sends it around the body, where is gets stored in muscles and in the liver as glycogen. When you exercise, you use this stored supply. The more you exercise, the more your body stores - one of the many adaptations that training creates.

But even the best trained athletes have a physical limit. The body can only store so much energy - in my experience (and I am far from alone) just enough to get you to the 30km point of a marathon. Running out of glycogen is what we fondly term "hitting the wall", "bonking", "carrying the piano", or "piggybacking the elephant". And those terms are not even exaggerations - your body literally runs out of petrol and your power output drops by what feels like 90%.

There are two strategies available
* Prevention
* Mitigation

## Prevention
Just, don't run out of fuel? Sure. So point one, the better trained you are, the more fuel your body will have (and the more efficiently it will use it). But if even the best runners will run out, what else can we do?
Eat.
Ingest carbohydrates, and have your body refuel while you are running. This is why runners eat gu's, have carbohydrates drinks, drink coke, eat boiled potatoes etc etc. The goal is to just get as much carbs into your body while you are running intensely as you can. But be sure not to upset your stomach. Shoving things in your mouth while you are running is not the hard part, having the gut that can digest it is the hard part. This too requires training. And as we will cover in race prep - nothing new on race day. This means either taking your own fuel to eat, or practising with what will be provided on race day beforehand. Rob de Castella had an unfortunate race day incident when he tried eating something new during a race that didn't agree with him. It was not pretty.

Note that the point of prevention is not to wait until you hit the wall to start eating - you will want to start early (even before the race starts) and consume relatively evenly throughout the event. (Although if you do have the misfortune of meeting a wall, eating will help get things back on track).

[This](https://marathonhandbook.com/how-sabastian-sawe-fueled-his-sub-two-hour-marathon-london/) is a super interesting read about how Sebastian Sawe fuelled his sub two hour marathon, to give an idea of how serious this aspect is in elite events.

## Mitigation
When you run out of carbohydrates, you slow down, but the lights stay on. You can still walk and talk and breathe. This is probably because your body doesn't let you use all the fuel you have, but also because you have an alternate fuel source - fat.
While the body will preference carbs, if there are none available it will use fat to power exercise. With training, fat can be almost as effective an energy sources as carbs are - but most runners almost never train using fat as a fuel. This is because when carbs are in your body, that is what will be used. In order to target fat as an energy source, you need to deplete yourself entirely of carbs - either by not eating them in the first place (think the keto diet, atkins diet, lchf, etc), or by doing your long run fasted. ie, avoid carbs at dinner the night before and then do your long run in the morning, avoiding all carbs. If your body's store is low enough, and you run far enough, you will force it to start using fat. And like any training, the more you do it, the more your body will adapt to make it efficient.
With enough practice, your body will become proficient at switching between the two energy sources, greatly reducing the consequences of running out of carbs.

## When to think about fuelling?
If you have done reasonable training, any race under an hour should be fine without the need to fuel. 

For races between 1 and 1.5 hours you might want to consider fuelling, but it is not critical.

For races over 1.5 hours, I recommend definitely thinking about what and when to eat. Practice a few different types of food and / or drinks during your long run and see what you like. Then think about how often you want to consume them, and whether to measure that by time or by distance. Make sure what you train with is what you race with - ie, if the race provides a certain brand or mix of energy drink / gel, either train with that or carry your own on race day!

Note that the gel companies (surprisingly!) will recommend that you buy and eat a lot of gels. Don't feel obligated to follow their recommendations. Like anything, start small and gradually increase until you reach the minimum effective dose. 

(Side note - keep an eye on the caffeine content of some gels! If you eat a few without thinking, you might be taking on the equivalent of 8 or 9 coffees!)

# The Central Governor Theory
Tim Noakes explains part of training adaption and the way the body works in [Lore of Running](https://openlibrary.org/books/OL22076478M/Lore_of_running) by his theory of a Central Governor.

His theory is that part of the brain acts as a safeguard when we are exercising, preventing us from being able to exercise our way to death. This part of the brain is quite cautious, and as we undertake strenuous exercise, it will limit the amount of the bodies resources the exercise is allowed to take. This will force the body to slow down, and / or stop, to prevent us going near the point where we might endanger ourselves, such as not having enough energy available for the brain.

One of the convincing arguments is that endurance athletes in long events (marathons, ironman triathlons etc), could be pushing themselves at the limit of what they believe is physically possible for hours - and yet, when they see and hear the finish line, the feeling of fatigue vanishes, and they are able to almost sprint the last kilometre. How and why is this possible? The body didn't get any extra resources. The athletes were trying as hard as they could already. The only thing that changed was the brain knowing that the end was in sight - so maybe the Governor knows it is now safe to allow the body to use more resources, because the body is not in danger.

Noakes believes that interval training is a key method of teaching the Central Governor that we can exercise at high levels of intensity safely (ie, without dying) - and so the next time we run at that level of intensity, the Governor will allow us more of the bodies available resources, and we can run further and faster before it will throttle us. 

Under this model, intervals are training part of your brain, as well as your body, which I think is super interesting.

# Running form
Running form refers to how you hold and move your body when you are running. From is a combination of things like your posture, stride length, foot strike. Everyone has their own personal style, and some schools of thought are that you shouldn't pay attention to form, your most efficient form for your unique body will naturally emerge as you run more. I agree with this to some extent, but have also discovered (and seen) a few ways you can definitely do it wrong, so let me offer a few pieces of advice.

Form is important because it will impact how efficiently you move forward, and whether you are unnecessarily increasing your risk of injury

# The core
Good running form requires a strong, stable core. When running, your arms and legs should move, the rest of your body, not so much. Think of trying to run on sand vs on the road. The road is much easier because you have a strong, stable object to push off against. The sand is squishy and moves around, and costs you energy that is wasted instead of converted to forward motion. 

The same applies to your core. The more stable your core is, the more efficiently your legs can push you forward.

A strong core will also help you keep good posture, which is critical. Your torso should be fairly upright, shoulders wide, chest open, head upright - this will maximise the effectiveness of your lungs and breathing. 

## Stride length / cadence
Stride length refers to how much distance you cover with each step. Cadence and how many steps you take in a given time (usually per minute). These are related because you could cover 100 metres in 20 seconds with 100 steps of 1 metre and a cadence of 300, or you could cover the same distance in the same time using 20 steps of 5 metres, with a cadence of 60. ie, for a given speed, bigger strides means lower cadence and vice versa.

Story time: when I started running as a kid, my theory - which felt entirely logical at the time - was that the person who took the fewest steps would win, because bigger steps meant covering more ground faster. So I focused on taking as few steps as I could, making my strides as long as possible - inadvertently bounding along with huge vertical motion and landing heavily with each step. Surprisingly, this was a terrible idea - one that I am still struggling to undo today, and I suspect they're behind a good portion of the injuries I've accumulated since. Do as I say, not as I did. 

Anyhow, thinking about how you would run if you didn't have shoes vs how you run in shoes, the most noticeable difference is that you would greatly reduce your stride length, and increase your cadence. To give an idea, most elite runners run at a cadence of around 180 - but of course they also have a reasonable stride length, because they are going so fast! A shorter stride will prevent overstriding, is generally more efficient, and most importantly, reduces the impact of each stride.

### Overstriding 
Overstriding is taking steps that are too large. More specifically, when you foot lands in front of your centre of gravity. This causes a couple of problems.
* Applying the brakes at each step. Think about what happens when your take big steps that start well in front of you: you're repeatedly extending your foot out in front of your centre of mass and then putting your full weight onto it. From a momentum perspective, this is like applying a small brake with every single step. Your weight is now ahead of you, and you have to work to get up and over it before you can drive forward again. Do this tens of thousands of times in a race and you've wasted a huge amount of energy that could have been spent on moving forward instead
* Vertical movement. In order to take these big steps, you are much more likely to bounce up and down higher than you would taking smaller steps. Given races are usually horizontal, vertical movement doesn't really help and should be minimised. Think about the maths- lets say you take 40,000 steps in a marathon. If each step incurs 10cm of vertical movement, thats 4 kilometres of vertical - almost half way up Mount Everest! Or over 1000 flights of stairs - in addition to the 42 kilometres of horizontal movement! Imagine racing against someone else that only moved 8cm vertically each step, they would remove 800m (approximately 300 flights of stairs) of vertical climb from their race!
* The impact of impact. Related to vertical movement, the amount of force your body must absorb with each step is directly related to the height you are landing from. While the impact of a single stride is (hopefully) very small, the cumulative impact through your bones, joints, and tendons grows large. Reducing your vertical movement from 10cm to 8cm reduces this cumulative impact by 20%. To give you an idea, average runners will have around 10cm vertical per stride, while elite marathoners will be [about half that](https://runnersconnect.net/improve-your-vertical-oscillation-for-better-running-performance/).

A more efficient stride is to land close to your centre of mass, and have your stride push out behind you. This removes the braking effect, and is likely to encourage less vertical motion and therefore less impact. For most of us office workers that sit all day, we will need to stretch our hips flexors to allow a longer stride behind us.

## Foot strike
Closely related to stride length, foot strike describes which part of the foot you land on. 
Runners that overstride are forced to land on their heels, and this is called heel striking. It has some associated problems described above.

Runners that land underneath their body are more likely to land with a flat foot, or on the middle part. This is a mid foot strike, and is considered the ideal.

Some runners manage to land on their toes. I have deliberately tried to figure out how they do this but can't. If you can manage it, its called toe striking, and places a lot of strain on your calves and achilles, so is probably not ideal.

## Barefoot running
Like many, I was pretty inspired by [Born To Run](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Born_to_Run_(McDougall_book)), and the idea that we have evolved for millions of years to learn to run without shoes, but have only been wearing shoes for ~100 years. It seems logical that our bodies would naturally be more suited to running barefoot. However our environment no longer really lets us - concrete, broken glass, other rubbish makes it hazardous where most of us live. However, this doesn't mean we can't learn anything from this line of thinking. Find a patch of grass — somewhere you're confident is free of anything that'll cut your feet — and go for a short jog without shoes. Pay attention to how you land. Notice what feels comfortable and what doesn't. Try varying your pace and observe how your stride naturally changes. Most people find, almost immediately, that they start taking shorter steps, their cadence picks up, and they become noticeably lighter on their feet. The reason is obvious once you experience it: without cushioning, landing heavily hurts. So your body stops doing it. Shoes, specifically cushioned shoes, stop this feedback, and allow you to run in a way that is unnatural for your body. Instead of getting direct feedback every step, shoes allow you to mask these problems... until they no longer can, and you get injured.
It makes sense to me to think of what would be a natural stride if you were barefoot, and trying to preserve that style even when you are wearing shoes.

## Glutes
The [Gluteus Maximus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gluteus_maximus) is the largest muscle in the entire body, and is designed to move you forward. However, the modern lifestyle of sitting and being immobile for most of our time means that many people have under-active glutes. I strongly advise throwing some [glute activation exercises](https://www.runnersworld.com/uk/training/cross-training/a773632/glute-activation-exercises/) into your routine, and then consciously trying to activate them when you are running.

## Hip Flexors
Hip flexors are the muscles at the front of your hips that connect to your thighs. How far you can stride behind you is likely limited by the flexibility of your hip flexors. Sitting in a chair where your thighs are of 90 degrees to your torso shortens these muscles. Sitting in a chair for most of the day, every day leads to a lot of shortening on these muscles, which will limit your ability to do the push part of the stride behind you. Gradually and consistently stretching your hip flexors will allow a more natural and efficient stride.

## Pronation
Pronation refers to the "rolling in" of the feet during the landing phase of each stride. Pronation is sometimes demonised, and targeted as a problem that needs to be "fixed" by specific footwear. However pronation is a perfectly normal feature of the body, and designed to help absorb of the shock of each foot strike. Without pronation, the impact will be taken on by the bones and other tendons and ligaments. 

Having said that, it is possible to over pronate, which can cause problems. This is an area I am not super familiar with, so if you are concerned you should seek professional advice. Ideally from a specialist that is also a runner or knows how 'special' runners are.
