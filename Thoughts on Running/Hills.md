# Hills
Hills are the third of the key workouts, and deliberately so. While hill sessions generally contribute less than either long runs or intervals, they are still very valuable. 

Having the strength to take on hills without having your body freak out feels really good. Hills are also a good way to strengthen your muscles and tendons without needing to run too far.

Most races have some hills, and some seem to deliberately seek out as much as they can ([six foot](https://www.sixfoot.com/caves-house-finish-elevation-and-map), [coast to kosci](https://coasttokosci.com/course/), [uta](https://uta.utmb.world/races/UTA100), [utmb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultra-Trail_du_Mont-Blanc#Route) ) - so if you have a specific race in mind, definitely check out the profile and determine how much hill training you should incorporate.

Hills can be combined with either long runs or speed sessions (somewhat). A long, hilly run can work both dimensions, or short hill reps can (somewhat) help with both speed and strength.

For most casual runners, incorporating hills somewhere in your regular weekly runs will be enough. Just don't dodge them forever, because one day you'll get caught out!

## How to get started
So you've decided you want to be a strong runner. What not?
As always, start small and build gradually but consistently. 
### Option 1 - Hills as a session
If you want to add a hill specific session, the approach is similar to an interval session, but on a hill.
* Find a hill which is something between 50 and 100 metres long and has a 'decent' gradient. 
* The gradient should be enough that you know it's a hill, but doesn't have to be super steep. Ideally a consistent incline for the duration. 
* Given the natural variation of hills, you don't need to find a perfect one. Just find one that is convenient and is in the ballpark of the description above.
* Start small and slow - maybe 2 or 3 reps, depending on the steepness, length and speed you want to run.
* Do a warm up jog of 10-15 minutes.
* Run up the hill
	* Start with a jog (depending on the steepness of the slope, maybe a fast jog)
	* Focus on holding your form and being strong
	* Keep your head and torso up, shoulders open so your lungs have lots of air
	* Use your glutes and calves to power you forward
* The ideal pace is a similar goal to intervals, you want all your reps to be roughly equally paced, and you want to feel pretty tired by the end
* If you can't hold your form, or you are massively out of breath, you need to slow down. This is a strength session, if you can't keep the right muscles engaged, you won't get the benefit
* Once you finish the rep, either walk or jog back, and repeat
* The next week you can go a bit harder and / or add a rep depending on how you felt after your last session. 

### Option 2 - Hills as part of other sessions
* Not a lot of runners will do a specific hill session, but they will ensure to have plenty of hills in their other sessions
* On your long run, start planning it to include some hills
* When you are on the hills, remember to focus on your form and being strong
* Make sure you keep your head and torso up, and are using your glutes and calves
* The more hills you cover throughout your sessions, the stronger you will become
* If you only have one or two hills available, nothing stops you making that a loop that you repeat a few times in the middle of your run
* Prioritise the hill effort. You will gain more from pushing up the hill and then slowing down to recover than from slowing down as you climb the hill
